Introduction

We're running all tournament battles again through multiple simulations to figure out team win rates. The number of simulations per match chosen has been n = 200.

We are conducting these win rate statistics over n simulations to achieve the following objectives:

  • Explain Matches: Help elucidate the dynamics of matches within a given tournament.
  • Identify Statistical Upsets: Better understand what constitutes a statistically significant upset.
  • BRS Upsets: When a team has significantly higher TOTAL BRS than the opposing team, yet the opposing team has a higher win rate.
  • Evaluate Synergies: Assess how certain synergies are performing relative to expectations.
  • Alignment with Broader Simulations: Compare and contrast performance with broader simulations encompassing various types of Gotchis.

Given our current computational resources and cost considerations, conducting 200 simulations per match has proven to be consistent. For instance, the differences observed in a given match across multiple simulations have not exceeded 1% win rate points. For example, a team that initially had a 199-1 score against another team in the original simulations might exhibit a 200-0 or 197-3 score upon rerunning the simulations.

For a BRS Upset to be detected and shown in a table, a team must have over 300 TOTAL BRS more than the opposing team, and the opposing team must have a higher win rate.

For a Statistical Upset, we have determined that if over n simulations, a team has over 90% win rate versus another team, and yet loses, this constitutes an upset.

There's a third table that detects cases where both teams have similar TOTAL BRS (within 50 points), yet the win rate difference is substantial (90%).

It's important to detect why these are happening, if it's because of synergy, leaders, traits, general team compositions etc, as they can constitute points of imbalance.
